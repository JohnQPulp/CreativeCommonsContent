Yesterday I published the visual novel for Mark Twain's Pudd'nhead Wilson:

That it's an entertaining and funny read goes without saying for Twain. But in addition to that, it also has interesting interesting first-edition illustrations, now all public domain. I wasn't aware of them at first, since I was reading the boring, pictureless Standard Ebooks copy of the novel, but I found that other site after starting the research for the book, which led to incorporating many of the original drawings into the VN.

But really, that an illustrated edition of the book existed shouldn't have surprised me. Twain novels are a treasure trove for original illustrations, with one of his illustrated novels in particular holding a special place for the site. You see, my profile picture on Github is this drawing:

Which comes from Twain's funniest novel, A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court:

More specifically, I pulled the profile from the Project Gutenberg high-quality scan of the illustrated edition of the book. And from that same book, I also pulled a couple more pictures:

These pictures are the character sprite reference and background picture reference I use when doing the image generations for this visual novels (alongside a big boilerplate prompt). In a future post I'll run through my full process for creating the visual novels from start-to-end, but at least as far as the visuals go, it's these two images that the VNs' art style is based on — the public domain art of Twain's writings.

And last month, while driving home for Christmas, I had the chance to stop over at the Mark Twain museum in Hannibal, Missouri:

In a cruel twist of fate, all of exhibits were open except the one for Connecticut Yankee, which was roped off for fresh paint:

Well, kind of roped off. I could still squint over at the plaques.

And in any case, it was further back in the museum, on the upper floor, that I found what I was really after — the original originals for the illustrations of the book!

From these great illustrations, made over 130 years ago, we have the basis for the visual novelization revolution all public domain literature. Though ironically, Connecticut Yankee itself will be one of the hardest VNs to make, since it will have to live up to the greatness of its original.
