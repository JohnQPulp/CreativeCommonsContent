Final Thoughts

This was a largely negative roundup, but I wouldn't say Garp is a bad book per-se. The prose is good and even funny in parts, but probably not as many parts nowadays as the author intended. It suffers more than other books as a product of its time in a few big ways:

First, there's all the writings-within-writings and writer characters and meta writing commentary. I guess this seemed more interesting back in the day, before Hofstadter blew all such works out of the water (the very next year!) with GEB. But as Garp stands now, I got annoyed basically every time the book reminded me how many of its characters were writers.

Second, the book is just too deeply tied to the specific politics of the 70s — 2nd-wave feminism and Taxi Driver turbulence — to be particularly insightful or compelling to the modern reader. In Irving's 40-year edition introduction to the book, he says:

Garp is a political novel, and the politics of sexual intolerance and suppression haven’t gone away.

Which is true, but he also writes:

In 1976–77, when I was living in Massachusetts and Vermont, where I finished Garp, it was inconceivable to me that the sexual violence I was writing about would long endure. In short, I thought sexual discrimination was too backward and too stupid to last.

Which makes him come across as an idiot, if he really thinks nothing has changed between 1978 and now. Or maybe he does think things have changed, but he was expecting perfect sexual harmony by now? Which is… also not very compelling as a political perspective. (Or the other other interpretation, which is that perhaps the 40-year intro he wrote was a quick cash-grab, which I could respect more.)

But in any case: Third and last, the book suffers form being gross in the way that everything from the 70s is gross. Which is a lesser complaint, and shouldn't be considered a deal-breaker for valueing works of that decade. But I think I'll still prefer to get my rapey political commentary from Zardoz, if I have to choose one.
